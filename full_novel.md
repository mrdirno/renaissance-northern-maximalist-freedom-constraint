## The Gilt and the Groaning Table
The first thing you must understand is that the world is not a sphere. It is a great, groaning table, and we are the feast laid upon it. The second thing is that I, Elara Vane, was born not with a silver spoon in my mouth, but with a stolen trowel in my hand, and I have been digging my way out of the dirt ever since.

On the morning the sky fell, I was up to my elbows in gilt.

The commission was a monstrosity of taste, a merchant’s country house outside Haarlem where the only rule was *more*. More fruit, more foliage, more fat-cheeked putti clutching at more drapery. The ceiling of the great hall was to be a vision of Olympus, but the merchant, a man whose fortune smelled of cloves and desperation, wanted his own face on Zeus. I was painting the thunderbolt, a twisting, foolish thing of gold leaf and ultramarine so expensive it made my teeth ache. The scaffold beneath me was a rickety kingdom of planks and rope. From this height, the marble floor was a distant, cold shore.

“Elara! The clouds on the west wall are too grey. My wife says they look like English weather.”

I didn’t look down. I pressed another wafer of gold onto the wet size, breathing the sharp, holy scent of it. “Your wife has never seen English weather, Mijnheer De Witt. She thinks a mist is a personal insult. Tell her they are the clouds of divine contemplation. Charge her extra for the philosophy.”

A grunt from below. De Witt was a bull in a brocade doublet, pacing the periphery of my domain. He feared the scaffold but feared more the idea that his money was not buying perpetual sunshine.

My brush moved, a tiny sable spear against the vast plaster sky. This was the alchemy: to take egg yolk and ground stone, pig hair and plant juice, and conjure a god. A fraudulent god, but a convincing one. My Zeus had De Witt’s jowls but my father’s eyes—a trick of the trade, a little vengeance in the vermilion. The trowel of my birth was metaphorical, but the tools of my ascension were literal: brush, chisel, pigment, lie.

The lie today was that I belonged here. A woman, twenty-seven, unmarried, directing a team of surly apprentices and journeymen on a commission of this scale. My hair, the colour of old port, was knotted and pinned under a linen cap, dusted with a fine snow of plaster and lapis lazuli. My hands were a map of my work: stained with umber, nicked by chisels, calloused by the grindstone. I wore a painter’s smock over a simple wool dress, practical, unadorned. The only richness was in the colours under my nails.

“The boy from the docks is here,” called Jan, my head apprentice, from the far scaffold. “With the shipment.”

“Which boy? Which shipment?”

“The ugly one. The amber.”

My heart did a quick, hopeful jig. True amber, fossilized sunlight, ground for the glazes of the gods’ robes. I had paid for it with three small, secret paintings sold under a man’s name to a collector in Antwerp. “Send him up.”

“He’s got a crate, Elara. He’ll bring the whole temple down on us.”

“Then tell him to leave it below and bring me a sample. And Jan? If you call him ugly again, you’ll be painting cherub backsides for the next month.”

I heard the scuffle below, the low murmur of voices. The light in the hall shifted as the great oak door opened. A slice of the flat, silver Dutch day cut across the painted forest on the south wall. Then the door closed, and we were back in our eternal, candle-lit twilight.

The boy who appeared at the edge of my platform was not ugly. He was thin, all elbows and Adam’s apple, with the wary eyes of a creature that lives on wharf pilings. His cap was in his hands. He clutched a small linen pouch.

“For the painter Vane,” he whispered, as if the painted gods might overhear.

“I am Vane.” I held out my hand. He placed the pouch in it. The linen was damp from his palm. I loosened the drawstring and poured a little mound of powder into my own hand. It was the colour of honey, of summer ale, of a shallow sea with the sun beneath it. I brought it close to my face. It smelled of pine forests and deep time.

“It’s good,” I said, more to myself than to him. “From the Baltic?”

“A ship from Königsberg. The master said you’d know.” He shifted his weight. “There’s… a letter. With the crate.”

A letter. No one wrote me letters. Clients sent summons. Colleagues sent insults or begging notes for loans. I felt a cold finger trace my spine. “From whom?”

“He didn’t say. A gentleman. Foreign. He paid the captain to add it to your crate.” The boy’s eyes darted to the pouch. “He said you’d want it.”

I closed my fist around the amber. “Bring the crate to my workshop. The canal-side door. And tell no one.”

“The gentleman said that, too.”

When he was gone, the pouch a warm weight in my belt, the great hall felt different. The painted clouds seemed heavier. De Witt’s Zeus looked less like a father of gods and more like a frightened, jowly man staring at a coming storm. I finished the thunderbolt, the strokes automatic, my mind racing down muddy canals towards my workshop, towards that crate.

My workshop was a ten-minute walk from De Witt’s folly, in a part of Haarlem where the houses leaned towards each other like gossips, their stepped gables cutting jagged teeth into the sky. It was a narrow, three-story wedged between a bakery and a cooperage, so the air was perpetually flavoured with warm bread and the tang of wet wood. The ground floor was the shop—a bold fiction, as I rarely sold directly—cluttered with samples of marble, bolts of cloth for drapery studies, and a few finished panels of fruit and flowers, my bread and butter. The second floor was the studio, north-facing, the walls a chaotic mosaic of sketches, studies, half-finished canvases, and anatomical drawings. The top floor was where I slept, cooked, and hid my money under a loose floorboard.

The crate was inside the workshop door, a plain, salt-stained thing. Next to it stood Lijsbet, my model, muse, and occasional cook, shelling peas into a bowl with violent efficiency.

“A man brought that,” she said, not looking up. “Big. Smelled of cold places.”

Lijsbet was from Ghent, with hair the colour of a carrot and a temper to match. She had a face that could launch a hundred realistic genre paintings—the serving girl, the market vendor, the sorrowful Magdalene. She sat for me when funds were low and my ambition was high.

“Did he say anything?”

“He asked if the painter Vane lived here. I said the painter Vane *worked* here. There’s a difference.” She popped a pea. “He looked like trouble. The expensive kind.”

I fetched a crowbar from the corner. The wood of the crate screamed as I pried the lid open. Inside, nestled in sweet-smelling straw, were bricks of pure amber resin, wrapped in oilcloth. A fortune in my hands. And atop them, a letter.

The seal was plain red wax, no insignia. The paper was thick, expensive. I broke the seal.

*Mistress Vane,*

*A man who knows the weight of light seeks a master of its illusion. Your Jupiter in The Hague (the one with the regrettable tapestry) speaks of a hand that can lie with truth. Your Magdalene in Antwerp (the one sold as a Van der Weyden) whispers of a spirit that chafes at constraints.*

*There is a room in Bruges. A private commission. The subject is… singular. The reward is beyond your current imaginings. It is not merely gold, but a kind of freedom.*

*If you are curious, present this letter at the House of the Swan, Klein Sint-Amandsstraat, before the feast of St. Luke. Come alone. Tell no one.*

*Yours in anticipation of collaboration,*

*A Patron*

I read it twice. The blood in my ears was a roaring tide. *A man who knows the weight of light.* That was alchemist talk, philosopher talk. *Your Jupiter… Your Magdalene.* He knew. He knew the forgeries, the secret sales, the works I’d done under other, dead men’s names. The threat was as elegant as the compliment. *A kind of freedom.* The words were a hook, baited with the one thing I could not resist.

“Well?” Lijsbet had stopped shelling. “Death warrant or love letter?”

“A bit of both, I think.” I folded the letter, my fingers leaving sweaty prints on the fine paper. The feast of St. Luke was the patron saint of painters. Two weeks away.

“You’re going,” she stated, seeing the answer on my face.

“I have to finish De Witt’s ceiling.”

“You’ll finish it in half the time now, and it’ll be twice as good, because your mind is already in Bruges.” She stood, wiping her hands on her apron. “I’ll pack your good brushes. And the knife you keep under your pillow.”

That night, in my attic room, I took down the small, framed panel from above my bed. It was not a masterpiece. It was a portrait of my father, painted by me when I was sixteen, just after his hands grew too shaky to hold a brush. He had been a painter of modest talent but immodest vision, a man who believed colour was a moral force. He taught me to grind pigment, to see the bone beneath the skin, to understand that every painting is an argument between what is and what could be. In the portrait, his eyes were clear and fierce, the only part of him the palsy had not touched. They were the eyes I gave to all my fraudulent gods.

“A private commission,” I whispered to the painted face. “A singular subject. Freedom.”

His eyes offered no counsel, only a challenge. He had died in debt, his name forgotten. I had sworn to live in possibility, my name known, even if it had to be whispered.

I placed the panel back on the wall. Outside, the bells of St. Bavo’s marked the hour. The sound washed over the rooftops, a cold, bronze tide. I thought of the crate of amber below, of the letter in my belt, of the ceiling in De Witt’s hall that needed a few more days of divine lies.

The world is a table, and I was no longer content to be part of the feast. I would be the one holding the knife. The one carving my own name into the wood.

I blew out the candle. In the sudden dark, the afterimage of the amber glowed, a tiny, captive sunset in my mind.

## A Lesson in Humility, Paved in Mud
The road to Bruges is a lesson in humility. It is a flat, wet ribbon of mud and ambition, cutting through a landscape so meticulously tamed it feels like a argument against God. Every inch is claimed, diked, drained, and planted. There are no wild surprises here, only the relentless, green productivity of money. I preferred the chaos of cities, where human ambition piled upon itself in brick and timber, where you could get lost. Here, under the immense bowl of the Low Countries sky, you were exposed. A solitary figure on a hired horse, with a crate of amber and a head full of dangerous thoughts.

I had told De Witt a family illness required my immediate presence in the south. I saw the suspicion in his piggy eyes—he knew a lie when he heard one, being a connoisseur—but the ceiling was nearly done, and the remaining work was for apprentices to fill in clouds and drapery. I left Jan in charge, with strict instructions and the threat of artistic damnation. I told Lijsbet to sell two of the smaller fruit panels if I wasn’t back in a month, and to use the money to pay the rent or flee, depending on the news.

The letter was a coal in my purse. *The House of the Swan, Klein Sint-Amandsstraat.* I repeated it like a prayer, a curse, a map to a cliff’s edge.

Bruges, when it finally rose from the mist, was a city of ghosts and money. Once, it had been the beating heart of the North, its canals thick with wool and wealth. Now the sea had silted, the trade had drifted to Antwerp and Amsterdam, and Bruges had settled into a beautiful, melancholic stillness. Its wealth was fossilized, like my amber. Its spires were grey fingers pointing at a heaven that had moved on. It suited my mood perfectly.

The House of the Swan was not a tavern, as I’d half-expected. It was a *godshuis*—a almshouse—a quiet, brick-built courtyard surrounded by tiny, identical houses for the pious poor. A charity. My patron was a philanthropist? Or a man who liked to hide in plain sight, amidst the scent of cabbage soup and piety.

An old woman in a severe wimple answered the gate. She took my letter without a word, her eyes scanning the script. They were the colour of flint.

“You are expected,” she said, her voice like dry reeds. “He is in the garden. Through the arch. Do not touch the roses.”

The courtyard was a geometric dream of clipped box hedges and gravel paths. At its centre, a stone fountain murmured to itself. And beside it, a man stood, his back to me, examining a white rose bush as if it held the secrets of the universe.

He was tall, wrapped in a travelling cloak of good, dark wool, unadorned. His hair, from what I could see, was iron-grey, swept back from a high forehead. He held himself with the easy, unconscious authority of someone who has never had to bargain for his supper.

“Mistress Vane.” He did not turn. “You are punctual. A virtue in women, so they tell me, and a necessity in artisans.”

His voice was educated, flavoured with an accent I couldn’t place. Not quite French, not quite German. It was the voice of castles and libraries, of borders crossed without question.

“You paid for my punctuality, sir,” I said, staying by the arch. “The amber was a persuasive clock.”

At that, he turned. His face was a surprise. He was older than I’d imagined, perhaps sixty, but his features were sharp, ascetic, carved by intellect rather than time. His eyes were a pale, watery blue, the kind that see through things. He looked like a scholar, or a particularly fastidious assassin.

“The amber is for the commission,” he said. “Not a bribe. A tool. I require the best tools.” He gestured to a stone bench. “Sit. We will talk of light and lies.”

I sat, perching on the edge, my boots gritty with the road. He remained standing, a dark column against the white roses.

“You know my work,” I began.

“I know the work of ‘Master Hendrik,’ and ‘the workshop of the late, great Floris,’” he said, a faint, cold smile touching his lips. “I have a eye for hands. Yours has a particular… tension. A struggle between precision and passion. It is what makes your forgeries so compelling. They are not slavish copies. They are conversations with the dead. You argue with your masters, and sometimes, you win.”

I felt stripped bare, as if he’d peeled back the layers of pseudonym and paint to the raw girl underneath. “You want a forgery?”

“No.” The word was final. “I want an original. A portrait. But the subject cannot sit for you.”

“A posthumous portrait? From descriptions? Those are guesswork. Honoured guesswork, but—”

“The subject is alive.” He cut me off, his pale eyes fixing on mine. “But cannot be seen. Not by you. Not by anyone who will speak of it.”

A cold trickle of understanding. “You want me to paint someone in secret.”

“I want you to paint *from* a secret.” He reached into his cloak and drew out a small, leather-bound book. It was old, the leather stained and supple. “This is your subject. Study it. Live with it. Then, you will paint not what you see, but what you *know*.”

He handed me the book. I took it. It was heavy, not with pages, but with consequence. I opened it carefully.

The pages were not paper, but vellum. On them, in ink of faded brown, were drawings. Not sketches, but *records*. Exquisite, terrifying, meticulous records of a human body. A man’s body. But not as a painter would see it. As a surgeon, or a devil, might.

Here, the musculature of the arm, every fibre laid bare. Here, the weave of the intestines, coiled like serpents. Here, the chambers of the heart, sliced open like a fruit. Here, the skull, from every angle, with measurements in a tight, precise script. The drawings were breathtaking in their detail, their brutal, unflinching clarity. They were the map of a man, from the inside out.

I looked up, my throat dry. “What is this?”

“The truth,” he said simply. “The subject is a… philosopher of the flesh. He has devoted his life to understanding the machine of the body. These are his studies. Of himself. Of others. He believes that to know the soul, one must first know the cage that holds it.”

“He dissects,” I whispered, a sacrilege.

“He *learns*,” the man corrected. “And he wishes for a portrait. Not of his face, which is ordinary. But of his… essence. The union of the mortal machine and the immortal spark. A painting that shows the man, whole, but informed by all this hidden truth. A portrait of a soul that knows its own plumbing.”

I stared at the vellum. The drawings were not ghastly. They were beautiful, in a horrifying way. The love in the line work was undeniable. This was devotion, a sacred profanity. “You want me to use these… to paint a living man? To imply all this… *knowledge* beneath the skin?”

“Yes. In a single, full-length portrait. He will be clothed, of course. In the robes of a scholar. Standing in a library. But the painting must *hum* with this inner truth. The set of his shoulder must speak of the clavicle beneath. The turn of his head must hint at the cervical vertebrae. The light in his eyes must suggest a mind that has held its own heart in its hands.” The patron’s voice grew low, intense. “It is the ultimate portrait, Mistress Vane. Not an exterior likeness, but an interior monument. A Renaissance of the self, in paint.”

The ambition of it stole my breath. It was insane. It was glorious. It was heresy, art, and philosophy hammered into a single panel. “And the freedom you mentioned?”

“The fee will be five hundred gold guilders.”

The number hung in the air, a solid, golden fact. It was more money than I would see in ten years of De Witts. It was a house. It was independence.

“And beyond the fee,” he continued, “when the work is done, delivered, and approved, your name—your real name—will be inscribed on a plaque in the library where the portrait will hang. Not as a decorator, not as a forger. As Elara Vane, Master Painter. Commissioned by a private patron of singular discernment. Your Magdalene in Antwerp will be but a prelude.”

Fame. Not whispered, but declared. Bought, perhaps. But then, what wasn’t?

“What is his name? This philosopher of the flesh?”

“That,” said the patron, “is the final constraint. You will never know it. You will never meet him. You will work from this book, and from one other thing.” He pointed to the fountain. Beside it, I now saw, stood a long, narrow crate, like one used for transporting armour or tapestries. “There is a death mask in that crate. Taken from a living subject. It is his face. You will use it for the likeness. The book is his soul. The mask is his shell. You will marry them.”

A death mask from a living man. The chill of the garden seeped into my bones. “Why all the secrecy? If this is a noble pursuit of knowledge—”

“The world is not ready for his knowledge,” the patron said, his voice turning to ice. “The Church, the universities, the mob… they see only desecration. They would burn his books and him with them. This portrait is his testament, to be revealed only when the world has caught up to his mind. Perhaps after his death. Perhaps long after. You are not just a painter, Mistress Vane. You are a historian of the future. Now.” He clasped his hands behind his back. “Do you accept the constraints? The solitude? The silence?”

I looked down at the book in my hands. At the beautiful, terrible drawings. I thought of my father’s shaky hands, his forgotten name. I thought of the attic room, the smell of bread and turpentine, the endless, genteel struggle. I thought of five hundred guilders, and a plaque with my name on it.

The world is a table. Here was my chance to carve the wood.

“I accept,” I said, and the words felt like a vow, or a sentence.

“Good.” He nodded, once. “Your workshop is prepared. It is in a house on Spiegelrei, the Street of Mirrors. It is secure. You will tell anyone who asks that you are in Bruges to copy the van Eycks in the cathedral for a wealthy burgher. A boring, pious task. You will have no visitors. Your supplies, your meals, will be brought. You have until the first frost.”

“And you? How do I contact you?”

“You don’t. I will contact you. Now, take your book. Take your mask. Go to the house. The key is under the stone dove by the door.” He turned back to the rose bush, dismissing me. The audience was over.

I stood, clutching the vellum book to my chest like a child or a shield. I walked to the crate, heaved it onto my shoulder—it was lighter than I expected—and walked back through the archway. The old woman was gone. The gate stood open.

On the Street of Mirrors, the canal water was still, perfectly doubling the tall, narrow houses. I found the door, found the key under the stone dove. The lock turned with a well-oiled click.

Inside, it was not a workshop. It was a cell for creation. A single, large room with a huge north-facing window, already fitted with the best German glass. Canvases, panels, pigments, oils, brushes of every kind were laid out with military precision. A comfortable bed, a desk, a stove. Everything I needed. Nothing I didn’t. There were no mirrors.

I set the crate down. I placed the vellum book on the desk. I lit a lamp, for the day was fading.

Then, with a deep breath that smelled of new wood and linseed oil, I opened the crate.

Straw. And nestled within it, a face.

It was plaster, white and smooth. The face of a man in late middle age. High forehead, strong nose, a mouth set in a line of profound, calm determination. The eyes were closed, the lashes delicate ridges on the plaster. It was a handsome face, intelligent, serene. It gave away nothing of the mind that had mapped its own entrails.

I reached out, my fingers hovering over the cold plaster brow. I did not touch it. Not yet.

I opened the vellum book instead, to a drawing of a hand, the tendons like harp strings, the bones a cathedral of tiny arches.

Outside, on the canal, a swan glided past, its reflection a ghost in the dark water. I was alone with a dead-living face and the beautiful, monstrous truth of a man’s insides.

The constraint was absolute. The freedom, if I could seize it, would be terrifying.

I picked up a piece of charcoal. I began to draw.

## The Fever of Lazarus
The first week was a fever. I drew until my vision blurred and my fingers were blackened stumps. I drew the death mask from every angle: three-quarter profile, from above, from below, in the flat grey light of dawn and the golden lie of lamp oil. I drew until I knew the exact curve of the left eyebrow, the way the right nostril flared a fraction more than the left, the subtle, asymmetrical pull of a lifelong habit of thought on the mouth. I gave him the name “Lazarus” in my mind, for he was a man who had seen the other side of his own skin.

Then, I married him to the book.

I drew Lazarus with the skin peeled back from his forearm, the beautiful cables of muscle exposed, but posed as if reading a volume on his desk. I drew him in scholar’s robes, but with a ghostly overlay of the rib cage beneath, the heart a dark, significant shadow. I drew his serene, plaster face on a body rendered as a anatomical écorché, a fleshless man of wonder. These were blasphemies, but they were my path to the synthesis my patron demanded.

The meals arrived silently, left on a tray outside the door: good bread, cheese, stew, the occasional roast fowl. Once, a bottle of Rhenish wine. I ate at the desk, one eye on the drawings, tasting nothing. The world outside the great window—the barge traffic on the canal, the calls of the sellers, the bells marking the hours—receded into a distant rumble. My universe was twelve feet by twenty, and it contained a multiverse of flesh and bone.

The constraint became a strange kind of liberation. With no living, breathing, fidgeting subject to please, I was free to invent the truth. My patron did not want reality; he wanted a higher fiction, one built on a foundation of brutal fact. It was the ultimate challenge for a forger: to create an original so convincing in its depth that it felt more real than reality.

On the tenth day, I began the underpainting on a large oak panel, prepared with a ground of flawless gesso. I worked in *verdaccio*, the green-grey monochrome that would map the light and shadow, the architecture of the face and form. I blocked in the figure of Lazarus, life-sized, standing beside a reading lectern. His hand rested on an open book. His gaze was directed out of the painting, towards a window I would paint later, a source of cool, northern light.

I gave him my father’s eyes. I couldn’t help it. The calm, fierce intelligence in the plaster mask demanded them. It was my secret, my signature in the bones of the work.

It was on the afternoon I began to model the face in subtle greys that the first complication arrived.

Not a person. A sound.

A low, rhythmic *thump*, followed by a faint, metallic scrape. It was coming from the other side of the east wall. The house was part of a row, and I had assumed the neighbouring buildings were empty, or occupied by quiet burghers. This sound was neither domestic nor commercial. It was deliberate, isolated. *Thump… scrape. Thump… scrape.*

I tried to ignore it, losing myself in the delicate task of rendering the translucent shadow of the lower eyelid. But the sound was a worm in my ear. *Thump… scrape.*

After an hour, I slammed my brush down. I walked to the wall, pressed my ear against the cold plaster. Silence. Then, just as I pulled away: *Thump-scrape-thump.*

A code? A workshop? It was too irregular for a loom, too soft for a smithy.

Irritation flared. My concentration, my sacred isolation, was being violated by a phantom noise. I had no way to complain, no landlord to appeal to. My patron was a ghost.

That night, the sound infiltrated my dreams. I dreamed I was painting Lazarus, but his skin was translucent, and beneath it, the organs pulsed and glistened not from the vellum book, but from my own memory of butcher’s shops and dead birds. And beneath the *thump-scrape* was a voice, whispering measurements in Latin.

I woke sweating. The room was pitch black. The sound had stopped.

The next day, it began at noon. *Thump… scrape.* Slower now. More resonant.

It was the sound of digging.

The realization turned my blood to canal water. Someone was digging, on the other side of this wall. In a cellar? Was my cell for creation built over a grave?

I fought the panic. This was Bruges. People dug for foundations, for repairs, for hidden treasure they believed the retreating sea had left behind. It was none of my concern. My concern was the fall of light on a painted wool robe, the suggestion of a tibia beneath the drapery.

I turned my easel to face away from the wall. I sang old Flemish folk songs my father taught me, loudly, off-key, to drown out the digging. I worked on the book on the lectern, painting in the illusion of tiny, precise text—a nonsense script of my own invention that hinted at profound learning.

But the digging was a toothache in my mind. It was the one thing I could not control, the variable my patron had not accounted for.

On the fifteenth day, the meals stopped.

The tray did not appear at noon. By evening, my stomach was a clenched fist. I opened the door a crack. The narrow hallway was empty, the street door locked from the inside as always. No tray. No explanation.

Had my patron abandoned me? Had he been discovered? Was the food a part of the constraint I hadn’t considered—to see how hunger sharpened my vision?

I had some hard bread and an end of cheese left from the previous day. I ate them sparingly, washing them down with water from the pitcher. The digging continued through the afternoon, a maddening counterpoint to the growling of my stomach.

Fear, cold and practical, began to seep in. Five hundred guilders were no use to a starved corpse. A plaque with my name on it was a fine epitaph, but I preferred breathing.

That night, emboldened by hunger and a rising, furious anxiety, I decided to explore. The house had two floors. My workshop was on the first. Above was a locked door. I had assumed it was empty, or contained supplies. Now, I needed to know.

I took my painting knife—a sharp, flexible blade—and a candle. The lock on the upstairs door was simple. A few minutes of clumsy probing with the knife, and the mechanism yielded with a rusty shriek.

The room was a shock.

It was not a storage room. It was a library. Or a tomb for books. Floor-to-ceiling shelves held hundreds, perhaps thousands, of volumes, all bound in identical, unmarked vellum. The air was thick with the smell of old paper, dust, and something else—a faint, sweet-chemical smell, like cloves and salt.

In the centre of the room stood a large table. On it lay not books, but instruments. Not painter’s tools. Surgical tools. Or perhaps alchemical ones. Scalpels with bone handles. Probes of polished silver. A small saw. A series of glass vials, empty and clean. They were laid out on a linen cloth with a reverence that felt religious.

This was *his* space. The philosopher’s. Lazarus’s real library. My patron had given me a cell below the lion’s den.

I stepped closer, the candle flame trembling. My eye caught a spine on a shelf that was not uniform. I pulled it out. It was a sketchbook. I opened it.

More drawings. But these were different. They were landscapes of the interior, yes, but annotated with frantic, marginal notes in the same tight script as the vellum book. Observations not just of structure, but of *function*. Next to a drawing of a lung: *“The bellows of the spirit—air becomes soul?”* Next to a study of a liver: *“The alchemist’s furnace—black bile the residue of thought?”*

This was not just anatomy. It was a mystical cartography. He was searching for the soul in the spleen, the mind in the marrow.

*Thump.*

The sound was directly below me now. In my workshop. No—under it.

*Scrape.*

I froze, the sketchbook in my hand. The sound was not next door. It was *underneath*. There was a cellar.

I replaced the book, fled the library, and closed the door behind me. Downstairs, in my workshop, I pushed aside the large rug beneath my easel. There was no trapdoor. The floor was solid, wide planks of oak. I knelt, put my ear to the wood.

Silence. Then, a long, slow, dragging sound. Like a sack being pulled across dirt.

Someone was down there. In my house. Or under it. Digging, and now… moving something.

The constraints of the commission shattered. This was no longer about art and freedom. This was about a locked house, a missing meal, a digging thing in the dark, and a library of knives upstairs.

My patron had not just hired a painter. He had locked her in a puzzle-box with a monster.

I needed to get out. But the street door was locked from the inside with a key I possessed. I could leave. But to do so was to abandon the commission, the guilders, the plaque, my one chance at the life I clawed for. It was also to step into the unknown of Bruges, where my patron clearly had eyes.

And if I stayed… what came up from the cellar when the digging stopped?

I looked at the painting on the easel. Lazarus’s grey-toned face gazed serenely towards his unseen window. My father’s eyes looked back at me, fierce, unafraid.

*The world is a table,* they seemed to say. *And you are holding the knife.*

I walked to my supply chest. I took out the largest, heaviest mortar and pestle, meant for grinding pigments. It was solid stone, a blunt, stupid weapon. I placed it by my bed.

Then I went back to the easel. I picked up my brush. My hand did not shake.

*Thump… scrape…* from below.

I mixed a little more grey. I began to paint the shadow under Lazarus’s jaw.

Let the devil dig. I had a man to build from the bones out. And I would not be the one who flinched first.

# Chapter 4

## A Map Written by a Liar
The first thing you must understand is that a map is not a guide. It is a confession, written in the hand of a liar who fears the truth. The second thing is that I, Elara Vane, had traded a digging thing in a cellar for a different kind of excavation, one that promised not gilt but rot, and I was beginning to suspect they were the same currency.

Three days after I left the house on the canal—the mortar and pestle left untouched, the cellar door never opened, the commission for Lazarus abandoned to the dust and the thing beneath the floor—I arrived in a town that clung to the edge of the land like a barnacle to a sinking ship. It was called Haven’s End, which should have been my first warning. Places that advertise their conclusions are rarely hospitable to new beginnings.

The journey was a blur of hired carts and suspicious glances. I’d taken only my paints, my tools, the guilders sewn into my coat lining, and the heirloom—the cool, carved weight of it a constant touchstone against my ribs. The map led here, to this wet, whispering place where the wind smelled of peat and forgotten things.

Elias lived on the far side of a salt marsh, in a house that seemed less built than excavated. It was a low, turf-roofed thing, half-swallowed by the hill, as if the earth were patiently reclaiming one of its own. There were no fat-cheeked putti here, only the skeletal frames of old fishing nets hung like giant, desiccated webs. The door was opened before I could knock.

He was a man of perhaps sixty, with the lean, corded strength of a root pulled from stubborn ground. His eyes were the colour of the slate sky, and they took me in with a single, sweeping assessment that felt less like sight and more like calibration.

“You’re the painter,” he said. His voice was a dry rustle, like pages turning in a sealed room. It wasn’t a question.

“I was hired to paint. Now I’m following a map.”

“A distinction without a difference. Both are trades in translation. Come in. The damp is not a friend to paper, or to women with stolen trowels.”

The inside of the house was a cave of knowledge. Not the ordered, weaponized library of my patron, but a chaotic ossuary of it. Scrolls jutted from barrels like bones. Books formed precarious stalagmites on the floor. Charts of strange coastlines were pinned to the walls, overlaid with transparent sheets of vellum scrawled with frantic annotations. The air was thick with the smell of old ink, mildew, and a sharp, metallic tang I couldn’t name.

He did not offer me a seat. He simply stood amidst the wreckage of history and pointed a bony finger at the satchel I carried.

“Show me the confession.”

I unwrapped the heirloom. In the grey light filtering through his single, thick-paned window, the carved bone seemed to glow with a sickly inner light. Elias did not touch it. He leaned close, his breath stirring the dust motes, and let out a long, slow exhale.

“The Canticle of the Deep,” he murmured. “I wondered which of the set would surface first. They usually do, when the Table gets hungry.”

I kept my voice flat. “The world is a table. I’ve heard the sermon.”

“You’ve heard the catechism. You haven’t lived the mass.” He finally looked at me. “Your patron. He is a member of the Feast of the Unseen Saint. A society of collectors, of connoisseurs. Their taste runs not to art, but to silence. They acquire truths, box them, and bury them in the cellars of the world. This,” he gestured to the heirloom, “is a key. One of seven.”

“A key to what?”

“To a lock that should never be opened. Or so they believe. The Canticle is a set of navigational instruments, carved from the bones of the first cartographers who tried to chart the places where God’s attention… wavers. Each piece points to a fragment of a greater map. A map to the *Tabula Veritatis*—the Table of Truth.”

A cold laugh escaped me. “More tables. Is everything a piece of furniture to you people?”

“Everything is a surface,” he corrected, his slate eyes sharp. “Some are for dining. Some are for dissection. The *Tabula Veritatis* is said to be the original. The blueprint. The Feast believes possessing it, controlling it, is the ultimate act of curation. They have spent centuries gathering the pieces, and centuries more ensuring no one else can follow the crumbs. Your Lazarus commission was a test. A beautifully set trap. They wanted to see if you had the… constitution for excavation. If you could build a man from bones, perhaps you could dismantle a myth. They also wanted you in a locked room, where your digging would be private, and your disappearance would be an easy footnote.”

The pieces clicked into place with a sound like a coffin lid settling. The locked house. The missing meal. The digging. Not a monster, but a *mason*—one of the Feast’s agents, planting something or retrieving something from a vault beneath my workshop. I had been living atop a reliquary.

“Why me? Why a painter?”

“Because a forger sees the world as layers,” Elias said, turning to a cluttered desk. “Pigment, ground, varnish, lie. You understand that reality is a palimpsest. The Feast needs restorers. Not of art, but of pathways. The heirloom you found is a compass. But it doesn’t point north. It points to the next piece. And you, Elara Vane, with your stolen trowel and your eye for falsehood, are now the needle.”

---

He called it training. I called it drowning in a tide of heresy.

We did not leave his buried house for two days. He taught me not how to fight, but how to read. The Feast, he explained, communicated through a symbology of consumption. A repeated fruit motif in a tapestry wasn’t decoration; it indicated a safe house. A specific arrangement of dishes on a painted banquet table could be a warning or a set of directions. Their codes were in the art they commissioned, the architecture they funded, the very menus of the inns they owned.

“They believe they are setting the table for eternity,” Elias said, as we pored over a reproduction of a Last Supper, his finger tracing the shadow of a knife. “Every locked door, every buried truth, every silenced witness is another course secured. You are not just a painter fleeing a patron. You are a crumb that has fallen from the edge. And they will sweep you up, or they will sweep you away.”

He made me translate. He would give me a page from a mundane-looking merchant’s ledger, and I would have to find the pattern in the debits and credits, revealing a shipping route for contraband texts. He showed me how the gilding on certain church altars, when viewed at a specific angle at dawn, cast a shadow-map of the city’s underground waterways.

My head ached with the effort. It was like learning to paint all over again, but this time, the subject was the invisible scaffolding of the world. He was a harsh, impatient teacher, but I was a desperate student. I had seen the under-side of the table. I knew what crawled there.

On the evening of the second day, he placed a small, flat object wrapped in oilcloth before me. “Your trowel is for digging in dirt. This is for digging in lies.”

I unfolded the cloth. Inside was a disc of polished obsidian, the size of a large coin, set in a simple pewter frame. On one side was a finely etched compass rose. On the other, a minuscule, perfect lens.

“A lodestone for the hidden world,” he said. “The lens will magnify details in paint, in stone, in paper. The compass… hold it.”

I palmed the disc. It was warm. After a moment, I felt a faint, insistent tug, like a fishhook in my mind. Not towards the door, or the window, but downwards, and slightly to the north-east. The same direction the carved bone heirloom seemed to hum against my skin.

“It points to the next piece of the Canticle,” I said.

“It points to the next *truth*,” he corrected. “The pieces are just the bones. The truth is the marrow. And the Feast wants to keep it locked in the larder, forever.”

---

The morning of my departure was a grey, wet affair. The marsh mist had slithered up to the very door of his house. Elias handed me a new satchel, stocked with dried meat, a water-skin, and blank vellum notebooks. “Write everything. In code. Use the system I showed you. Your memory is now a vault they would love to crack.”

I stood at his threshold, the weight of the obsidian disc in one pocket, the bone heirloom in the other. I felt like a walking contradiction, a collection of fragments pointed at a hole in the world.

“Why are you helping me?” I asked him finally. “You could take the heirloom. You know more than I do.”

Elias looked past me, into the swallowing mist. For the first time, I saw not a fierce scholar, but a tired old man, bent from a lifetime of carrying secrets.

“I was a restorer, once. For them. I believed in the curation. In the preservation of dangerous knowledge. Then I learned that a truth preserved under lock and key is not a truth at all. It is a specimen. A trophy. They are not feasting to sustain themselves. They are gorging to prove they can. I am helping you,” he said, his rust-voice dropping to a whisper, “because someone must spill the wine. Someone must overturn the plates.”

He gripped my arm, his fingers strong and cold. “But remember this, painter. The Feast has many servants. They wear the faces of patrons, of priests, of fellow travellers. They offer shelter, companionship, aid. Their poison is not always in the food. Sometimes it is in the hand that offers the bread. Trust the compass. Trust the bone. Trust your eye for the false layer. Trust nothing that breathes.”

It was an exit line, stark and grim. I nodded, turned, and stepped into the mist. It closed around me like a wet shroud, muffling sound, erasing the path behind me. I walked for an hour, the obsidian disc a steady, pulling warmth in my hand, leading me towards a road only it could see.

By the time the mist began to thin, Haven’s End was a ghost behind me. The road ahead was a muddy track between skeletal hedgerows. I was alone, armed with a stone that pointed to secrets and a warning that painted every human shape as a potential knife.

I made camp at the edge of a copse of wind-bent trees as dusk bled into a starless night. I ate sparingly. I did not light a fire. The silence was immense, broken only by the drip of moisture from the branches. I took out the obsidian disc and held it. The pull was stronger now, a clear, magnetic yearning towards the dark line of hills on the horizon.

I was digging again. But this time, I was not in a locked room with a monster below. I was in the open world, and the monster was the table itself, and every hand upon it might be setting a place for my end.

I wrapped my hand around the disc, its edges biting into my palm. The cold was a comfort. It was a truth, simple and sharp.

*Let them feast,* I thought, staring into the perfect black of the obsidian, where my own reflection was swallowed whole. *I am not on the menu. I am the trowel in the garden. I am the crack in the cellar wall. And I am digging for the sun.*

Somewhere, far to the north-east, in the hills the disc yearned for, a dog barked. The sound was swallowed by the night almost instantly.

But it was not the bark that froze the blood in my veins.

It was the answering whistle that came a moment later—clear, melodic, and human. It was the same three-note tune the merchant’s servant had whistled, emptying my water jug in the house on the canal.

The Feast had not waited for me to reach the next course.

The waiter was already here.

# Chapter 5

## Where the World Lets Its Edges Fray
The first thing you must understand about a border is that it is rarely a line. It is a sigh. A place where the world grows tired of holding its shape and lets the edges fray. The map Elias had pressed into my hand marked the wilderness with a neat, inked cross-hatch, as if danger could be contained by careful penmanship. The reality was a gradual surrender. The skeletal hedgerows gave way to a thick, sucking bracken that clutched at my boots. The muddy track dissolved into a confusion of animal trails. The air lost the damp, peat-scented memory of farmland and grew sharp with pine resin and stone-dust. I was not crossing a boundary. I was being absorbed by a deeper, older hunger.

By midday, the disc in my hand was no longer a gentle pull. It was a hook. It pointed, unswerving, into the throat of a narrow gorge where a river roared white over black rocks. This was the treacherous landmark, not a thing to navigate around, but a thing I was required to swallow.

Elias’s teachings had been scraps, flung to me in the dark of his cellar: *Listen to the water’s complaint. The loudest path is for the tourists. The true one whispers.* I stood at the gorge’s roaring mouth, spray cooling my face. The obvious route was a slippery ledge above the torrent. Suicide. I closed my eyes, letting the thunder vibrate in my teeth, and listened for the whisper.

Behind the curtain of sound, I found it. A smaller, persistent trickle, not from the river, but from the moss-cloaked wall of the gorge itself. I pushed through a veil of hanging ivy, my stolen trowel-hand knowing the give of false growth. Behind it, the rock face was weeping. A seam of moisture traced a vertical crack, and beside it, worn to a shine by time and not by tools, were foot-smears in the stone. A staircase for goats and ghosts. The hidden path.

I took it. The world narrowed to the pressure of granite under my palms, the cold kiss of seepage on my cheek. The roar of the river became a distant, irrelevant fury. Here, in the cleft, it was only my breath and the insistent, warming pulse of the obsidian against my chest. I was a stitch being drawn through a rip in the world’s fabric.

The crack delivered me to a high, wind-scoured ledge. Before me lay the barrier, and it was not a thing Elias could have prepared me for.

The gorge ended. The river plunged into a mist-filled void. And on the other side of that gap, perhaps thirty feet across, the cliff face rose again, sheer and flawless. Except it was not flawless. Carved into it, a ladder of ancient, shallow handholds and toe-scrapes ascended into the low cloud. They were old, so old the rock itself seemed to have grown around the idea of them. They led up and out of sight.

There was no bridge. Only a single, thick root, gnarled and grey as a dead nerve, spanning the chasm. It was slick with spray. It groaned in the wind.

My blood was not frozen now. It was a hot, shameful drumming in my ears. This was not a test of skill, but of offering. The table was demanding a sacrifice of certainty. I could turn back. The crack in the rock would take me. I could return to the world of merchants and ceilings and feasts I understood. I would live a long, painted life inside a gilded frame.

I looked at the obsidian disc in my palm. In its black mirror, the sky was a thin, grey ribbon. No reflection of my face. It had already swallowed me. I had been digging for the sun, and the sun, it seemed, was on the other side of this.

I did not think of falling. Thinking is the enemy of the climb. I thought of the three-note whistle in the dark. A waiter, clearing a place. I would not be returned to the menu.

I sheathed the trowel at my belt. I tucked the disc away. I walked to the end of the root where it tore from the cliff, put my hands on its rough, damp skin, and stepped out into the air.

The world became a symphony of strain. The root bowed. My boots slithered. The mist from the waterfall below coated everything in a cold sweat. I did not look down. I looked at the next handhold on the far cliff, a dark indentation like a missing tooth. I moved one foot, then the other, a slow, sideways betrayal of the void beneath me. The wind plucked at my coat, a beggar wanting my attention. I gave it nothing.

When my grasping hand finally closed on the far side of the gorge, the rock was warm. As if the sun had been baking it for days, though no sun had pierced this mist. I hauled myself onto the first handhold, my muscles singing with a terrible, clear truth. There was no going back. The root, as if released from a duty, gave a final, splintering crack and fell away into the white noise below.

Now, only the climb remained. Each hold was a puzzle of trust. I pressed my body to the stone, becoming a lichen, a stain. Fear was a luxury I poured into the abyss with each exhale. Upward. The cloud swallowed me. The world vanished into a pearl-grey womb. There was only the next grip, the next pull, the burning in my shoulders, and the warm, guiding heart of the disc over my own heart.

And then, my hand reached for a hold and found flatness.

I dragged myself over a final lip and lay on my back, heaving, on solid, level ground. The mist was thin here, a fading veil. I stared up at a colour I had forgotten.

Blue.

A vast, impossible, vaulted ceiling of cerulean blue, streaked with high, thin clouds. The sun hung in it, not the watery coin of the lowlands, but a fierce, sovereign jewel. Its light was a physical weight, a golden warmth that pressed the damp from my clothes and the chill from my bones.

I pushed myself up onto my elbows.

The sigh was over. The frayed edge had been crossed.

The landscape on this side of the cliff did not change dramatically. It shattered.

The ground was a tapestry of low, silvery grass and wildflowers of a violet so intense it hurt the eyes. It rolled in gentle swells towards those distant, beckoning hills, which now seemed closer, their outlines sharp as cut paper. But it was the sky that held the truth. It was too large. The proportions were wrong. This sky belonged to a grander sphere, a wider table. And the air… the air tasted of green lightning and the dust of cold stars. Every breath was a shock of clarity, as if I had been breathing smoke all my life and only now discovered lungfuls of crystal.

I stood. My legs trembled, but not from the climb. From the sheer, silent *pressure* of the place. It was special, yes. Hidden. But it was not a sanctuary. It was an amplifier. The feeling of being watched, which had been a prickle on my neck in the mist, was now a settled, focused gaze. The sun here did not feel like a comfort. It felt like a lamp held over a specimen.

I took out the obsidian disc. It was no longer warm. It was hot. Almost too hot to hold. The pull was not a yearning now, but a demand, a direct line pointing like a compass needle to a specific fold in the nearest hill. There was no more guesswork. The destination was clear.

I began to walk. The grass whispered under my boots. No birds sang. The only sound was the wind through the silver blades, a soft, sibilant conversation. I had walked perhaps half an hour, the strange sun at my back, when I saw the first sign that I was not the first to answer such a call.

Ahead, nestled in the dip where the land began to rise toward the hills, was a circle of standing stones.

They were not the grand, monolithic sentinels of legend. They were short, squat, and ugly, like rotten teeth jutting from the earth’s gum. Their surfaces were pocked and stained. And they were not empty.

From a distance, I took them for more stone, or perhaps strange fungi. But as I drew closer, my steps slowing, the truth resolved itself.

They were hats. Boots. Bundles of cloth.

Placed with care atop each stone was a piece of clothing. A weather-beaten tricorne, its leather bleached pale. A single, tall cavalry boot, scuffed and cracked. A shepherd’s smock, neatly folded. A child’s pinafore, faded but clean. Twenty stones, twenty offerings.

A shrine of absence.

I stopped at the edge of the circle. The disc in my hand was a burning coal. The pull came from beyond this place, but the circle demanded a toll of attention. This was not a marker left by the ones who made the handholds. This was something else. Something newer, and far more desperate.

I saw the glint then, in the centre of the circle. Not on a stone, but on the ground, resting in the cup of a natural hollow.

I stepped between the stones. The air grew still and thick. I knelt.

It was a spectacles case. Tortoiseshell, worn smooth at the edges. I opened it.

Inside, folded with precise care, was a pair of wire-rimmed spectacles. The lenses were clean. Beside them, tucked into the silk lining, was a small, handwritten note on good vellum. The ink was brown, not black.

I lifted it out. The handwriting was elegant, scholarly.

*“For the one who sees the path. May your vision be clearer than mine. I chose to remember the world as it was, not as it is. Do not follow.”*

It was signed with a single, elaborate initial: *R.*

I closed the case. The warning in my pocket, *Trust nothing that breathes*, echoed in the silent circle. But this was a warning from something that had ceased to breathe. A final, elegant act of defiance against the pull of the disc.

I stood, the case in my hand. An offering to an offering. I could leave it. I could take it.

The three-note whistle cut through the still air.

It came from behind me, from the direction of the cliff, clear as a bell in the strange, amplified silence. It was not the idle tune of a man at work. It was a signal. A confirmation.

I did not turn. Turning would be an acknowledgement. An acceptance of the chase.

I looked at the stones, their crowned absences. I looked at the spectacles case in my hand. A man named R. had reached this sacred, terrible place and had chosen to go no further. He had left his sight behind and walked back into the world, blind to its truth.

The waiter was at the border. He would cross the cliff. He would enter this amplified world.

And I was standing in a circle of abandoned identities, holding a dead man’s eyes.

The hook for the next chapter was not in the hills ahead. It was in the choice in my palm. I could drop the case. I could run towards the destination, the hot disc screaming in my grip.

Or I could do what I have always done.

I slipped the tortoiseshell case into my coat pocket, next to the cold trowel. I turned, not towards the hills, but to face the approach from the cliff.

Let the waiter come. Let him set his table in this silver grass.

He would find no docile guest waiting to be served.

He would find the trowel. And I had just been shown where to dig.

# Chapter 6

## The Grammar of an Ambush
The first thing you must understand is that an ambush is not a conversation. It is a period, forcefully inserted into a sentence that was not yet finished. The second thing is that I have never been fond of full stops.

The waiter did not come.

I waited at the edge of the silver grass, the trowel a cold promise against my ribs, the dead man’s spectacles a weight in my pocket. I had set my own table: a circle of standing stones, a sky of hammered pewter, a silence so profound I could hear the blood humming in my own ears. I was prepared to serve him a course of sharpened iron and unanswered questions.

But the three-note whistle was not followed by footsteps. It was answered.

From the hills ahead—the direction of the screaming disc—came a different sound. Not a whistle, but a low, melodic hum, like a bow drawn across the rim of a crystal glass. It cut through the amplified air and everything in it seemed to vibrate: the grass, the stones, the fillings in my teeth. The world held its breath for a single, stretched second.

Then the ambush descended. Not from the cliff, but from the sky.

They dropped from the low, scudding clouds on filaments of shimmering wire, silent as spiders. Three of them. Their clothes were the colour of dust and dried blood, close-fitting and devoid of any mark or fastening. Their faces were obscured by masks of polished bronze, featureless except for a single, vertical slit for vision. They carried no obvious weapons. Their hands were empty, fingers splayed, and that was more terrifying than any blade.

They landed in a perfect triangle around me, the silver grass whispering under their boots. They did not speak. They simply turned those blank, bronze faces inward, and the hum intensified, becoming a pressure behind my eyes. The disc in my satchel gave a sympathetic thrum, a pulse of heat against my hip. It recognized them. Or it called to them.

“I wasn’t aware I’d made a reservation,” I said, my voice sounding small and frayed in the humming stillness.

The one directly before me tilted its head. A voice emerged, but not from a mouth. It seemed to form directly in the air, genderless and cold, each word perfectly enunciated. “The artifact is not yours to carry, digger. Relinquish it.”

So they knew the trowel, too. I smiled, the expression feeling like a crack in porcelain. “You’ll have to itemize the bill. I’ve acquired several artifacts today.”

I moved, not away, but towards the standing stone at my back. My hand found the cold granite. The hum spiked, a needle of pure sound drilling into my temple. The one on my left raised a hand. The air between us thickened, warping the light like heat haze off a kiln. I tried to lift the trowel, but my arm was leaden, swimming through syrup.

This was not a fight. It was a foreclosure.

The one before me stepped forward, its hand outstretched for my satchel. I braced, a useless snarl in my throat. I would not make it easy. I would go down painting my defiance in the dirt.

The shot was a punctuation the silence did not deserve.

It was a sharp, wet *thwack*. The bronze mask of the figure reaching for me splintered. Not from a bullet, but from a small, ceramic pellet that burst on impact, releasing a cloud of viscous, grey gel. The gel seethed and hardened instantly, encasing the head in a stony shell. The figure staggered, the oppressive hum faltering.

A shadow detached itself from the lee of a larger standing stone thirty paces away. A woman. She moved with a liquid, economical grace, a long, rifle-like device braced against her shoulder. It was made of brushed steel and weathered wood, unlike anything I’d seen.

“Down, Vane!” she barked, her voice husky and threaded with command.

I dropped. Another *thwack*. The figure on my right caught its pellet in the chest. The grey gel exploded across its torso, crystallizing, locking its arms to its sides. The hum died into a ragged, discordant whine.

The third assailant turned its blank face towards the new threat. The air shimmered. The woman was already moving, rolling behind a stone as the space where she’d stood rippled and boiled, the grass beneath turning black and brittle.

I saw my chance. The pressure was gone. I surged up, not with the trowel, but with a handful of the hard, silver earth. I flung it at the remaining figure’s vision slit. It was a child’s move, a desperate, dirty trick. The figure flinched, the distorting haze wavering for a critical second.

The woman rose from her cover, her weapon now held like a staff. She closed the distance in three swift strides and drove the butt of it into the figure’s throat. There was a sound like a tea cup cracking. The figure collapsed, the hum ceasing entirely.

Silence rushed back in, louder than the noise before.

The woman stood over the fallen agent, breathing hard. She slung her weapon over her shoulder and turned to me. She was perhaps ten years my senior, her face all sharp angles and weathered competence. Her hair, the colour of old ash, was shorn close at the sides. A long, pale scar bisected her right eyebrow and trailed down her cheek. Her eyes were the colour of a winter sea, and they assessed me with a brutal, unflinching clarity.

“You’re harder to ambush than I was led to believe,” she said, nodding at the three immobilized forms. “But you’re walking into a slaughterhouse with a dinner bell.”

I got to my feet, brushing dirt from my knees. My heart was a frantic bird in a cage of ribs. “And you are?”

“Kaela.” She didn’t offer a last name. She nudged the nearest figure with her boot. The grey shell was unyielding. “They’re Consensus. The polite, academic name for a group of fanatics who want to iron all the wrinkles out of the world.”

“They wanted the disc.” I touched my satchel, the metal still warm.

“They don’t *want* it, Vane. They were sent to retrieve it. To put it back in its box. Or to put you in a box and take it.” She looked past me, towards the crowned stones. “You found R.’s offering.”

It wasn’t a question. I felt the shape of the spectacles case in my pocket. “You knew him.”

“I was his student. Before I saw what the Consensus truly curates.” She spat the word. “Control. Not preservation. Not understanding. They believe the truth of the disc—the truth of the Table—is too volatile. Too dangerous for the feast to know. They would silence the groaning timber and call it peace.”

Her words found the cracks in the little I knew, filling them with a cold, logical dread. The waiter, his elegant menace, was just a butler. These bronze-masked things were the house security. And the owner of the house wanted the world quiet, orderly, and blind.

“Why help me?” I asked.

Her winter-sea eyes held mine. “Because R. chose to remember. He walked away blind because he refused to serve their lie. And you… you’re the one who picks up the tools others leave behind. You’re a wrinkle they can’t iron out. I find that useful.”

It was not a comforting alliance. It was a transaction. I had something she needed: the disc, my stubbornness, my place on this path. She had something I needed: knowledge, and a weapon that fired silencing gel.

“The waiter,” I said. “The man from the cliff.”

“Lysander,” Kaela supplied, a flicker of disgust in her voice. “Their prime facilitator. He sets the table for the difficult guests. He was herding you here, to this open ground, for them.” She gestured with her chin towards the hills. “Their waystation is ahead. A place they call the Verge. It’s where they intercept and… process… those who get too close.”

“Then we go around.”

“There is no around. The path to the disc’s origin is through the Verge. It’s a choke point they built on a choke point.” She offered a grim, thin smile. “But they weren’t expecting a wrinkle. And they weren’t expecting me.”

She moved to the edge of the stone circle, peering towards the cliff. “We have minutes before Lysander arrives to find his clean-up crew mired in stone. We move. Now.”

We left the humming silence of the crowned stones for the whispering silence of the hills. Kaela set a punishing pace, her knowledge of the terrain evident. The land began to change. The silver grass gave way to low, twisted shrubs with leaves like obsidian shards. The air grew colder, sharper. The hammered pewter of the sky darkened to the colour of a bruise.

We travelled for an hour, speaking only when necessary. She pointed out almost-invisible markers: a spire of rock split in a perfect line, a patch of ground that echoed hollowly underfoot, a stream that flowed with water clear as air and just as tasteless.

“The Table’s old plumbing,” she said, when I stared at the water. “The Consensus studies it. Maps it. They’ve spent generations figuring out how the feast is laid out so they can decide who gets served, and who becomes the meal.”

I thought of the merchant who wanted his face on Zeus. The desire was the same, only the scale was different. “And the disc? The screaming?”

“A pressure valve,” she said, her voice low. “Or a alarm. A piece of the world remembering it’s a world, and not a… a diagram.” She glanced at me. “R. believed the discs were keys. Not to power, but to perception. They don’t unlock doors; they adjust the lens. The Consensus fears that. A changed perception is the one thing they cannot control.”

We crested a rise, and she pulled me down abruptly. Below, nestled in a shallow valley, was the Verge.

It was not a fortress. It was worse. It looked like a grand, open-air library that had been struck by lightning and fossilized. Slabs of dark, glassy stone—similar to the standing stones, but worked, shaped—rose in terraces and arcades. There were no walls, only columns and archways, creating a labyrinth of shadows and light. In the centre, a slender tower, like a needle of black ice, pierced the low sky. No movement was visible. It hummed, but with a deeper, more institutional frequency than the agents’ weaponry. A place of study, of quiet, terrible deliberation.

“The path,” Kaela whispered, pointing to a vast, natural arch of the same glassy rock at the far end of the complex. “It goes through there. The Consensus calls it the Lens. It’s the only stable route forward. And it’s guarded by more than bronze masks.”

“Guarded how?”

“By the Verge itself. It’s a puzzle box. The architecture is the lock. To pass through the Lens, you must align the perspective. And the key…” She trailed off, her eyes scanning the complex. “The key is a sacrifice of certainty.”

Before I could demand a translation less cryptic, a scuffling sound came from behind us. We both turned, Kaela’s weapon coming up.

A figure stumbled over the ridge, red-faced and gasping, a bulky pack threatening to overbalance him with every step.

Sam.

He saw the weapon and froze, his hands coming up. “Whoa! Don’t shoot! It’s me! I’m with the digger!”

I stared, a riot of disbelief and a strange, unwelcome relief battling in my chest. “Sam. What in the name of all that’s cracked are you doing here?”

He lowered his hands, still breathing heavily. “You left your sketchbook. The one with the cliff diagrams. And then that Lysander chap came sniffing around the inn, asking about a woman with a trowel. Seemed… off. I put two and two together.” He hefted his pack. “I brought supplies. And your book. Thought you might need both.”

Kaela’s weapon didn’t waver. “You led him here.”

“I did not!” Sam’s indignation was genuine. “I took the long way, the one the old charcoal-burners used. Saw those bronze buggers on the wires from a mile off. Hid until the fireworks started, then followed your trail.” He looked at me, his earnest, open face full of a concern that felt alien in this sharp, metallic landscape. “You’re in it deep, Elara. Deeper than gilt and ceiling plaster.”

Kaela studied him for a long, tense moment, then slowly lowered her weapon. “A liability,” she stated flatly.

“A cook,” Sam retorted, some spine showing. “And a decent judge of character. You haven’t shot her yet, so you’re probably one of the good ones. Relatively speaking.”

A faint, almost imperceptible snort escaped Kaela. It might have been amusement. “He stays quiet, he stays close. Or he becomes a liability we can’t afford.”

We waited until the bruised sky deepened into twilight. The Verge, in the failing light, became a cathedral of shadows, the black columns swallowing the last of the day. The hum seemed to grow louder in the dark, a subliminal vibration in the bones.

Kaela led us down, not towards the main entrances, but along a fissure in the valley wall that brought us out behind one of the lowest terraces. We moved through the columned arcades like ghosts. The place was not empty. Occasionally, we glimpsed robed figures moving between the slabs, carrying instruments or scrolls. Their footsteps made no sound. The air smelled of ozone and cold stone.

The path through the complex was a spiral, winding inevitably towards the central tower and the great arch—the Lens—beyond it. The puzzles began subtly.

The first was a hallway of columns. As we entered, the columns began to rotate slowly, their shadows twisting across the floor in impossible geometries. The way forward was clear, but the shifting shadows created a disorienting strobe. “Don’t follow the light,” Kaela murmured. “Follow the sound of my voice.” She began to hum a single, low note, holding it steady. We walked, eyes half-closed, trusting the sound, and emerged from the other side as the columns ground to a halt.

The second was a chamber with a floor of polished black stone that reflected the starless sky above perfectly. To walk across it was to walk on nothingness, a vertigo that clawed at the stomach. The solution was to close our eyes entirely and walk a straight line, guided by touch along a cool, almost invisible rail at waist-height. Sam, sweating profusely, muttered prayers to every saint he could remember.

We avoided the tower. It pulsed with a malevolent intelligence, and Kaela gave it a wide berth. “The Apex,” she said. “Where they do their calculations. Where they decide what truth is permissible.”

Finally, we stood before the Lens.

It was not an arch of stone, but of solidified shadow, a doorway of pure, depthless black framed by the glassy rock. Through it, I could see the hills continuing, but warped, as if seen through a flawed crystal. The air before it shimmered with heat-haze, though the cold was biting.

Before the Lens, the floor was inlaid with a magnificent, terrifying mosaic. It was a map of the world-as-Table, intricate and vast. But it was unfinished. At its centre, where the disc’s origin might be, was a void. A missing piece.

Set around the edges of the map were three plinths. On each sat an object.

On the left, a perfect, polished sphere of brass.
On the right, a jagged, raw shard of the same black, glassy stone that comprised the Verge.
On the central plinth, directly between the viewer and the void in the map, was a small, empty dish of tarnished silver.

Kaela’s face was grim. “The final alignment. The Consensus calls it the Trilemma. To open the Lens, you must complete the map. You must place a truth in the dish.”

Sam peered at the plinths. “So… put the ball in the dish? Or the rock?”

“It’s not a choice of objects,” Kaela said, her voice tight. “It’s a choice of perspectives. The sphere is perfect, whole, complete. The Consensus’s ideal. A truth that is smooth, contained, and ultimately a lie. The shard is fragmented, painful, incomplete. A raw, honest piece of a larger, broken truth.”

I saw it then. The horrible, elegant simplicity of it. “And the dish is empty because it waits for a truth from the traveler.”

Kaela nodded. “A personal truth. A piece of your own certainty. You must place it in the dish to fuel the Lens. The sphere or the shard are decoys. Offer one of them, and the Lens will reject you. The Verge will know you for a thief or a fool.”

Sam looked from the plinths to the shimmering black arch. “What happens if you offer your own… truth thing?”

“The Lens opens. And you walk forward having left a piece of your understanding of the world behind. It’s how they cull the curious. The weak turn back. The arrogant offer a decoy and are trapped. The determined…” She looked at me. “The determined walk forward lesser than they were.”

A sacrifice of certainty.

The humming of the Verge seemed to focus here, a questioning drone. My satchel grew warm. The disc knew it was home.

“What do I do?” The question was for myself as much as for her.

“You have to choose something you know to be true. Something foundational. And you have to give it up. Visibly. Irrevocably.” Her winter-sea eyes were bleak. “For R., it was his sight. He left his spectacles. He walked back into the world believing it was not as he saw it.”

I thought of my truths. That the world was a Table. That I was born with a trowel. That I could dig my way out of anything. Were they truths, or were they stories I’d painted on the ceiling of my own life?

I reached into my pocket. My fingers brushed the tortoiseshell case. R.’s surrendered sight. Then they closed around the cold, familiar haft of my trowel.

I pulled it out. The old, stained iron gleamed dully in the weird light. It was the first truth I had ever known. The weight in my hand. The promise of excavation.

Sam sucked in a breath. “Elara, no.”

Kaela was silent, watching.

This was the hook. Not in the hills ahead, but here, in this choice. The Consensus believed they could force the choice, could control the narrative of sacrifice. They believed I would either cling to my tool and be trapped, or surrender it and be neutered.

But I was a digger. And a digger knows that to surrender a tool is not to surrender the knowledge of how to use it. The trowel was an extension of my will, but my will was my own.

I stepped forward onto the mosaic Table. I walked past the perfect sphere, past the jagged shard. I stopped before the empty silver dish.

I did not place the trowel in it.

I knelt. I positioned the point of the trowel against the mosaic floor, right at the edge of the void in the map. I looked at the representation of the world, this beautiful, intricate lie of inlaid stone.

And I began to dig.

The sound was a shocking, violent scrape that tore through the institutional hum. Chips of precious stone flew. I was not completing their map. I was defacing it. I was adding my own, crude, undeniable truth to their perfect diagram: that a map is not the territory. That a digger with a trowel can always make a new hole.

I dug until I had pried up a single, rough, uneven piece of the mosaic—a fragment of blue lapis that might have been a sea, clutched in a setting of mortar and clay. It was ugly. It was real. It was a piece of their truth, violently extracted by my own.

I stood, my knees aching. I dropped the clump of shattered mosaic—*my* truth, that all constructs break—into the silver dish.

For a second, nothing happened.

Then the dish flared with a cold, blue light. The light shot in a beam to the Lens.

The arch of solid shadow dissolved.

Not into the warped hills, but into a vista that stole the breath from my lungs. A landscape of impossible, geometric mountains under a sky of swirling, liquid gold. The path continued, clear and sharp.

The Lens was open.

Kaela stared at me, a new, fierce respect in her eyes. “They will have felt that. The whole Verge will be awake now. They will come.”

A deep, resonant chime began to sound from the black tower, an alarm of profound violation.

Sam hefted his pack, face pale but set. “Time to go.”

I sheathed my trowel. It was still mine. I had not surrendered it. I had used it.

We ran through the arch, leaving the humming, chiming Verge behind. The air changed instantly. It was thinner, colder, charged with a strange, metallic energy. The geometric mountains seemed to watch our progress.

We ran until our lungs burned, putting distance between us and the archway, which from this side was a mere shimmer in the air, a tear in the world.

Finally, we collapsed in the lee of a slope that rose at perfect sixty-degree angles. The silence here was different. Not amplified, but *attenuated*, as if the world was holding its breath.

Kaela checked her weapon, her hands steady. “That was not in their manuals.”

Sam rummaged in his pack, pulling out a water skin and some hard bread. He handed me a piece. “Here. Eat. You just broke their favourite toy.”

I took it, my hands trembling now that the adrenaline was fading. I had done it. I had passed through. I had my trowel, the disc, R.’s spectacles.

But as I looked back the way we had come, at the shimmer that was the Lens, I felt the weight of the sacrifice nonetheless. I had not left a truth in the dish. I had left a declaration of war. A messy, violent, undeniable statement.

The Consensus now knew my name, my face, and my methodology. I was no longer a curious digger to be herded or silenced. I was a vandal in the sacred halls.

Kaela followed my gaze. “They won’t follow. Not immediately. The path from this side is one-way for them. It requires a sacrifice they are not willing to make. They’ll find another way. They’ll send Lysander. Or worse.”

“What’s worse than Lysander?” Sam asked around a mouthful of bread.

Kaela looked up at the liquid gold sky, her scar pale in its eerie light. “The ones who give him his orders. The Curators. They don’t work in the field. They work on the fabric of the field itself.” She turned to me. “You have what they want, Vane. And you’ve just shown them you know how to break their toys. The chase is over. Now the hunt begins.”

I finished the bread, the taste of dust and defiance on my tongue. I had my trowel. I had my wrinkle. And I had just dug a hole in the middle of their perfect map.

Let the Curators come. Let them try to smooth me out.

I would show them what grows in the broken places.

# Chapter 7

## Sanctuary as a Sharpening Stone
The first thing you must understand about sanctuary is that it is never a place of rest. It is a pause in the bleeding, a stone to sharpen your knife against. The second thing is that a labyrinth does not exist to keep you out. It exists to see what you are made of, and to feed on the leftovers.

We found the entrance not by sight, but by the silence it drank.

The geometrically perfect slope gave way to a scree of black, volcanic glass that chattered underfoot like broken teeth. Beyond it, the land folded into a series of razor-edged ravines, as if the table of the world had been scored by a furious god’s fingernails. At the heart of this scar tissue was a cleft in the rock, so narrow it seemed a mere shadow until you stood before it. From within breathed an air that was old, cool, and carried a scent like ozone and wet clay. It was the smell of a wound that never closed.

Kaela halted, a hand raised. She didn’t need to speak. The energy was a pressure against the skin, a low hum in the fillings of your teeth. It wasn’t the amplified, curated resonance of the Lens. This was deeper, wilder. A root-tone.

“The Sanctuary of the Unwritten,” Sam murmured, his usual bravado sanded down to reverence. He consulted a small, copper-bound journal—R.’s notes, salvaged from the ruin of his shop. “According to the fragments, it’s not a library. It’s a… tuning fork. For realities that didn’t make the final cut.”

“A graveyard for might-have-beens,” I said, my voice too loud in the swallowing quiet.

“Or a nursery,” Kaela corrected, her eyes never leaving the cleft. “The Consensus calls it a septic tank. A place where failed concepts collect and fester. They’ve tried to seal it a dozen times. It unseals itself.”

I hefted my pack, feeling the solid, guilty weight of the disc. This was where it was meant to be returned. A sacred object, back to its sacred hole. The irony was a knot in my stomach. I had crossed a world to become a delivery girl.

That’s when Sam, kicking at the glassy scree with his boot, let out a soft curse. “Hey. Look.”

It was half-buried, glinting with a forlorn, domestic sheen against the primordial black. A pocket watch. Not a Consensus chronometer with its fluid gears and silent sweep, but a brass-cased thing, the glass over its face cracked in a spiderweb pattern. Sam pried it up. It was warm, as if recently held. He flipped it open. The inside of the lid was engraved with a crest—a stylized owl perched on a key.

“The Hearth-Keepers,” Kaela said, her voice flat. “Lysander’s pet burglars. They’re sentimental. They always carry a piece of home.” She took the watch, her thumb brushing the cracked glass. “They’re already inside. And they’re ahead of us.”

The bread turned to chalk in my mouth. The chase was over, Kaela had said. Now the hunt begins. It seemed the hunt had a head start.

***

The cleft admitted us single-file, the rock whispering against our shoulders as if testing our width, our worth. For twenty paces, it was darkness and constriction, the sky reduced to a thin, golden thread far above. Then it opened, and the breath caught in my throat.

We stood on a natural ledge overlooking a cavern so vast its ceiling was lost in a haze of self-generated, phosphorescent mist. The light was the blue-green of deep ocean, cold and dreaming. And below, rising from the cavern floor, was the labyrinth.

It was not built. It was *grown*. Curving walls of a smooth, milky stone that looked like solidified moonlight spiraled out from a central spire, forming a pattern that was less a maze and more a fingerprint—the fingerprint of a giant. The pathways between the walls were not level; they dipped and rose, bridged by arches of the same living stone, and in places, they disappeared entirely into the mouths of smaller caves that pocked the cavern walls. The air thrummed with that root-tone, here so strong it vibrated in the chest.

“A tuning fork,” Sam repeated, awed. “Struck once, and still ringing.”

Kaela was already scanning, tactical. “They’ll be making for the center. The Spire. That’s where the stories say the Core rests.” She glanced at R.’s journal in Sam’s hands. “Any helpful hints? ‘Turn left at the third existential dread’?”

Sam flicked through the pages, covered in R.’s spidery script and frantic sketches. “He never got in. His notes are all second-hand, third-hand. Myths. He says the labyrinth doesn’t test your knowledge. It reflects your… composition. Your moral density, he calls it. The paths shift based on who walks them.”

“Wonderful,” I said, my stolen trowel a comforting weight against my hip. “So it’s a narcissist’s funhouse.”

“It’s a filter,” Kaela said. “The Consensus fears it because they can’t control the narrative here. Their truths are just more clutter on the floor.” She pointed. There, on the nearest pathway, a scuff in the fine, silvery dust that coated everything. A boot-print. Then another, leading inward. “They’re following a map. We’re not. Stay close. Don’t touch the walls.”

We descended a natural staircase of rock to the cavern floor. The silence was a physical presence, absorbing the sound of our footsteps until we moved like ghosts. Entering the labyrinth was like stepping into the conch of a vast ear. The hum clarified, resolved into layers—a distant, crystalline chiming, a low drone like stone grinding on stone, the faint, impossible echo of voices speaking no language I knew.

The first choice was a fork. The left path sloped downward, dark and inviting. The right climbed, bright with that internal phosphorescence. The boot prints went left.

Kaela hesitated. “Too obvious.”

“They have a guide,” Sam argued. “Maybe the obvious path *is* the right one if you’re carrying the heirloom. It’s a key, isn’t it?”

I looked down the left path. It felt like a throat. “R. said it tests your composition. What is the composition of a thief? Guilt? Greed?” I thought of the merchant who wanted his face on Zeus. “A sense of entitlement. The belief that you deserve to take.” I turned to the right, the climbing path. “What does it make of someone digging their way out?”

Without waiting for consensus, I started up. The stone beneath my feet was warm, almost pliant. After a dozen steps, the path curved, and the wall to my right grew translucent. Within its moonstone depth, shapes swam—flickering, half-formed images. A city with towers of woven light. A forest where the trees sang in harmony. A sea of liquid silver under a double moon. Possibilities. Worlds that had been imagined, perhaps even drafted, but never ratified. The might-have-beens.

“Don’t look too long,” Kaela warned, her voice tight. “It’s not a gallery. It’s a bait.”

I forced my eyes forward. The path widened into a circular chamber where five new arches led off in different directions. In the center of the chamber stood a statue. Or rather, it was a figure of the same milky stone, but it was not carved. It was *captured*. A man in Consensus explorer’s garb, his face frozen in a look of profound, ecstatic understanding, one hand outstretched toward the wall he had presumably touched. He was not dead. I could see the faint, slow pulse of light in his stone veins. He was listening. He was, perhaps, forever listening to the song of a world that never was.

Sam sucked in a breath. “Okay. Don’t touch the walls.”

We chose an arch at random, the one that seemed to lead most directly toward the central Spire, visible in glimpses between the curving walls. The labyrinth began to work. The second chamber had no statue, but the air was thick with the scent of a memory—for me, it was the smell of wet oil paint and linseed oil from R.’s shop. For Sam, he later said, it was the ozone of a lightning storm over the sea. Kaela showed no reaction. The third chamber presented a silent, looping performance of shadow-puppets on its walls, acting out a tragedy of betrayal and forgiveness that made my eyes sting.

It was a sieve, shaking us to see what was loose inside.

The fourth chamber was different. It was empty, just a small, round space. But as we entered, the archway behind us sealed with a soft, final sigh, not with stone, but with a curtain of solid shadow. The light from the walls dimmed.

And from the smooth floor, words began to glow, as if written in foxfire.

**STATE YOUR PURPOSE.**

The voice was not sound. It was the voice of the root-tone, vibrating in the marrow of my bones.

Sam flinched. Kaela’s hand went to her weapon. I stood still, my heart a frantic drum against my ribs.

“We seek to return what was taken,” I said, my voice small in the encompassing quiet.

The words on the floor shifted, reformed.
**THAT IS AN ACTION. NOT A PURPOSE. STATE THE WELLSPRING.**

Kaela’s jaw tightened. “To restore balance. To deny the Consensus a weapon.”

**THAT IS A REACTION. NOT A PURPOSE. STATE THE ORIGINATING FIRE.**

Sam, sweating, tried. “For knowledge! To see what’s in the Spire!”

**THAT IS CURIOSITY. A CHILD’S FIRE. IT GUTTERS.**

The words faded. The silence pressed in, accusatory. The labyrinth had found us wanting. It would not let us pass. We would be turned around, led out, or perhaps invited to join the listening statue.

Panic, cold and sharp, rose in my throat. This was the filter. This was the test of moral density. And we were failing. I thought of the stolen trowel in my hand, a tool for digging, for revealing what was buried. I thought of the messy, violent truth I had left in the Consensus dish. A declaration of war. Is that all I was? A vandal? A reaction?

Then I thought of the merchant’s ceiling. Of painting a god in the image of a fool. I had done it for the money, yes. But also for the secret, savage joy of knowing the truth of it—that the god was a fool, and only I, the painter, knew. It wasn’t admiration I sought. It wasn’t even freedom, not truly.

The realization rose, raw and unadorned.

“I’m tired of painting other people’s lies,” I said, the words leaving me like a confession pulled by forceps. “I’m tired of living in a world where the edges are sanded smooth and the cracks are filled with putty. I don’t want to restore balance. I don’t want to deny them a weapon.” I looked up, as if I could meet the gaze of the cavern itself. “I want to see what’s in the cracks. I want to find the patterns in the broken pieces. I want… I want to prove that the mess is the meaning.”

The silence held. The foxfire words on the floor died completely.

Then, with a sound like a mountain sighing in its sleep, a new archway opened in the wall that had been blank. Not leading toward the Spire, but deeper into the labyrinth’s heart, down a path that glowed a faint, welcoming gold.

The root-tone hummed, a single, approving note.

Kaela looked at me, her expression unreadable. “The originating fire,” she said, and it wasn’t a question.

Sam just shook his head, a ghost of a smile on his face. “Mess is the meaning. R. would have liked that.”

We stepped through the new arch. The path here was narrower, more intimate. The walls no longer showed might-have-been worlds. They showed fragments. A single, perfect leaf from a tree that never existed. A clockwork butterfly, frozen mid-tick. A tear, captured in crystal, containing a galaxy of sorrow. The debris of creation.

And at the path’s end, we found the Hearth-Keepers.

***

They were in a circular vault, where the root-tone was so strong the air itself seemed to shimmer. The vault was clearly a sanctum; its walls were lined with niches holding objects of heartbreaking strangeness—a bottle of solidified laughter, a compass that pointed to regret, a crown woven from last breaths.

The three Keepers were clustered around a simple plinth of rough-hewn rock in the room’s center. On it sat a small, unassuming clay bowl, filled with a liquid that held the blue-green light of the cavern. One Keeper, a woman with a severe braid, held our target—the heirloom, a disc of the same moonstone as the walls, but etched with a pattern that hurt the eyes to follow. She was lowering it toward the bowl, her face rapt.

She never completed the motion.

Kaela moved like the strike of a snake. No shout, no warning. Her weapon—a compact, crystalline rod—emitted a subsonic pulse that didn’t make a sound but hit the chest like a punch from a ghost. The woman with the braid gasped, staggered, and the disc flew from her hands, clattering across the stone floor.

Chaos, silent and efficient, erupted.

The other two Keepers—a broad-shouldered man and a younger woman—spun, drawing elegant, fluid blades that seemed made of darkness. Sam was already moving, not toward them, but in a wide arc, pulling a handful of gritty powder from his belt and hurling it into the air between us. It hung there, a sparkling curtain, and as the male Keeper charged through it, his movements slowed, as if he were pushing through deep water.

Kaela engaged the younger woman, their fight a ballet of brutal economy. The rod against the dark blade sparked with tiny, silent flashes of violet light.

My eyes were on the disc. It had skittered to a stop near the plinth. The braided woman was shaking off the effects of the pulse, pushing herself up, her hand reaching for it.

I dove. Not a soldier’s dive, but a digger’s lunge. My fingers closed over the cool, humming stone an instant before hers did. I rolled, coming up with the disc clutched to my chest, the world tilting.

The braided woman stared at me, not with anger, but with a profound, weary disappointment. “You don’t understand,” she hissed, her voice the first human sound in the sanctum, grotesque and small. “It must be cleansed in the Core! Its resonance is unstable! It’s drawing them!”

Behind her, on the plinth, the liquid in the clay bowl began to vibrate, its surface puckering with concentric rings. The root-tone shifted, warping into a discordant, hungry whine.

The vault’s entrance darkened.

Not with shadow. With a *lack*. A shape stood there, tall and impossibly slender, wearing a suit the color of forgotten grey. Its face was smooth, featureless but for two dark lenses where eyes should be. It held no weapon. It didn’t need to. The air around it died, the hum of the sanctuary flattening into a dead zone of static.

Lysander.

He had not come through the labyrinth. He had been *smoothed* into the space, a wrinkle forcibly ironed out.

“The disc, Vane,” he said, and his voice was the sound of pages being methodically torn from a book. “And the spectacles. You have broken enough toys for one day.”

Kaela and her opponent froze. Sam was pinned, the slowed Keeper’s blade at his throat. The braided woman looked from me to Lysander, her disappointment curdling into terror.

I stood, the disc in one hand, my other instinctively going to the trowel at my hip. The stolen trowel. The tool for broken places.

He took a step into the room, and the very light seemed to flee from him, leaving a trail of dullness in his wake. “The Consensus extends a final courtesy. A trade. The artifacts. For the preservation of this…” his head tilted, a minute, mechanical gesture, “…septic tank. Refuse, and we will not simply seal it. We will collapse the probability wave that sustains it. Every might-have-been in this cavern will become a never-was. A final, smooth edit.”

He was giving me a choice. A painter’s choice. Surrender the truths I held, or be responsible for the obliteration of a universe of possibilities.

I thought of the listening statue, forever enraptured by a lost song. I thought of the leaf, the butterfly, the tear. The mess. The beautiful, fragile mess.

Lysander extended a long, grey hand. “The composition of a vandal is simple, Elara Vane. It is ultimately, destructive. You dig, but you do not build. You break, but you do not mend. Give me the pieces. Let the professionals restore the canvas.”

The root-tone groaned around us, a sound of agony. The liquid in the bowl was boiling now, violently, though no heat came from it.

I looked at Kaela, her face a mask of grim acceptance. At Sam, his eyes wide. At the Hearth-Keepers, who had sought to cleanse a weapon and had instead drawn the exterminator.

I had my trowel. I had my wrinkle.

And I had a bowl of unstable resonance, vibrating on the plinth between us.

A professional restores the canvas. But sometimes, the only thing to do with a rotten canvas is to cut out the infected piece and see what’s underneath.

I met Lysander’s dark lenses. “You’re right,” I said, my voice surprisingly steady. “I don’t build. I don’t mend.”

I lifted the moonstone disc high, as if to surrender it.

And then I brought it down, not into Lysander’s hand, but with all my strength, into the center of the clay bowl.

The world did not explode. It *unfolded*.

A silent, concussive ring of pure possibility erupted from the plinth. It was not light, not sound, but a wave of *is-ness* and *is-not-ness* crashing together. The walls of the vault flickered, and for a heartbeat, we were not in a cavern. We were in a city of singing trees, under a double moon, by a sea of liquid silver. We were everywhere and nowhere, every draft reality contained in the disc flooding out in a desperate, final gasp.

Lysander staggered, his smooth grey suit rippling as if seen through a heat haze. He did not cry out. He simply… *fuzzed*. His edges became uncertain.

“Fool,” he whispered, the word fraying at the edges.

Then the wave hit the walls of the sanctuary, and the great tuning fork of the world was struck anew.

The cavern screamed. The milky stone of the labyrinth flushed with angry, bloody gold. The floor heaved. A crack shot up the wall behind the plinth, and from within the crack poured not rock, not light, but a torrent of *green*—vivid, violent, living green. Tangled vines, thick as my arm, bursting with thorns and strange, pulsating fruit, erupted into the sanctum, growing with impossible, voracious speed.

The Consensus’s smooth edit had failed. The seal was broken, not to be closed, but to be overgrown.

We were not free. We were fugitives in a world that wanted its truth locked away. But we had the map to a different world in our heads. And somewhere, in the gathering dark ahead, a fire was burning.

The feast was over. The digging had just begun.

# Chapter 10

## The Act of Subtraction
The first thing you must understand about running is that it is not an act of motion. It is an act of subtraction. You shed certainty, then plan, then breath, then thought, until all that is left is the hammer of your heart and the ground fleeing beneath you. The world, that great groaning table, becomes a blur of hostile edges. And you are not a person. You are a verb. A single, desperate syllable: *go*.

We went.

We heard the snap-crack of branches that were not branches, the low, resonant hum that was not insects but their devices seeking the echo of the Chamber’s energy in our bones. They were not tracking footprints. They were tracking the wound in reality we had just crawled through.

“They’re herding us,” Sam grunted, as we plunged down a shale-scree slope, our boots sending avalanches of noise into the twilight. “East. Towards the bluffs.”

“Exactly,” he said.

The determined drone of bees in the neglected corner of the garden, building a hive in a carved stone urn. The conversation was ongoing. The wild was whispering back. It would take time, but the house, too, would be reclaimed.

The constraint of the city—its rules, its greed, its desperate geometry—was no longer a cage. It was the very structure against which the resilient, rooting song of the world was composed. My freedom was not freedom *from* this place, but freedom *within* it. The freedom to hear, to translate, to plant a single, quiet note in the cacophony.

That night, the three of us sat in the cottage garden as the first stars pricked through the velvet dark. The air was sweet with turned earth and the herbal scent of the tea Kaela brewed. Sam was whittling a piece of wood into an uncertain shape that was becoming a bird.

“A letter came today,” Kaela said, her voice barely disturbing the twilight. “From Edinburgh. The master mason writes that the ‘acoustic principles’ in our treatise have solved a persistent damp problem in their oldest hall. He says the stones now sing in the rain, and the hall is dry for the first time in a century. He’s… curious for more.”

A smile touched my lips. A seed had sprouted.

“And a package from Naples,” Sam rumbled, not looking up from his whittling. “The baker. She sent… well, taste it.” He nodded to a cloth-wrapped parcel on the table.

I opened it. Inside were three small, perfect loaves of bread. We broke one open. It was dark, dense, studded with olives and herbs. We ate in silence. It tasted of sun on stone, of resilience, of a joy that was deep and quiet and nourishing. It tasted, unmistakably, like forgiveness.

We sat there, in the quiet symphony of the night—the rustle of the new leaves on the silver-leafed sapling that had grown from Silas’s staff, the chirp of crickets, the distant lap of canal water, the comfortable scratch of Sam’s knife.

I looked at my hands, resting in my lap. They were clean, but the earth was permanently etched into the lines of my palms. They were no longer just the hands that dug, or plastered, or fought. They were the hands that planted an acorn on a grave. That shaped a honest loaf of bread for a baker’s sign. That held the weight of a friend’s trust. They were my constraints, my tools, my proof.

The world is not a sphere. It is not a table.

It is a garden. A wild, tangled, endlessly growing garden. And we are not the feast. We are not the gardeners.

We are the seeds that remembered how to sing to the dirt. We are the mycelial threads whispering news between the roots. We are the slow, patient fruit.

The first thing you must understand is that the digging never ends. But the purpose of the trowel changes. You are not digging out. You are digging in. You are turning the soil over, making it soft and ready, for the next note, the next seed, the next fragile, necessary chord.

On the morning the sky remade itself, I was up to my elbows in soil. And for the first time, I was home.
